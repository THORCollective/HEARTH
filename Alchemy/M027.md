  A compromised internet-facing edge device (router, firewall, VPN, IoT) has been enrolled into an Operational
  Relay Box network and is relaying third-party traffic between external operators and downstream C2. Modeling each
  perimeter device's normal traffic shape surfaces the anomaly: a device that begins both accepting inbound

  before.

## Why

- **ORB networks are built to raise the cost of IOC-based defense.** Operators rotate through pools of compromised edge devices so that any single relay IP is short-lived; chasing IPs loses. Modeling each of *your own* perimeter devices' behavior flips the problem — you detect the compromise by the role change, not by matching the adversary's disposable infrastructure.
- **The relay role is the signal, not the ports.** Internet-facing devices receive odd inbound connection attempts constantly, so inbound-on-high-port alone is noise. What is anomalous is a device that begins *pairing* inbound sessions with symmetric outbound flows — accepting traffic and forwarding it on behalf of third parties. That inbound⇄outbound correlation is what a per-device model can learn and a static rule cannot.
- **Edge devices are under-instrumented and long-lived.** Routers, firewalls, and IoT rarely run EDR, so network flow is often the only telemetry — making a flow-based volumetric model the right (and sometimes only) sensor for on-device ORB behavior.
- **A cheap high-fidelity join exists.** UAT-7810's self-signed `exploit`-everything certificate and frozen spoofed User-Agent give a near-signature corroborator that promotes a scored flow anomaly into a confident alert without waiting for the next campaign's IOCs.
