## Why

- **Shape, not volume, is the signal.** A credential brute-force and a nightly backup both hammer one host:port — raw attempt-count can't tell them apart. What separates them is the _flow shape of a failed login_: a storm of TCP resets, tiny auth-failure replies, and short connect-fail-close flows, versus a benign flood that opens longer connections and transfers real data. Measuring the flood's shape per source->service window catches the brute force that a volume threshold buries.
- **A static attempt-count threshold fails in both directions.** Set it low and every backup job, health check, monitoring probe and CI runner that legitimately pounds an auth port trips it; set it high enough to silence those and a paced password-spray slips under. Only a multi-feature model — reset-rate, reply-size, duration alongside rate — separates "service that always floods" from "attacker guessing credentials."
- **Grounded in observable flow telemetry.** The reset storm and reply-size asymmetry survive encryption: even for SSH or TLS, the flow-level RST counts, byte asymmetry and durations are visible in Zeek conn.log, NetFlow with TCP flags, or CICFlowMeter/NTLFlowLyzer features — no payload inspection needed. BCCC-CSE-CIC-IDS2018 provides labelled SSH/FTP/web brute-force campaigns against a benign enterprise baseline to train and measure it.
- **Complements the identity-side hunt.** M001 baselines per-user login _patterns_ (unusual time/location/device) on authentication logs to catch account takeover after credentials succeed. M025 is the network-flow counterpart before they succeed: the flood of failed attempts itself, keyed on flow shape rather than user identity — useful exactly where per-user history doesn't exist (external scanners, service accounts, freshly exposed hosts).
