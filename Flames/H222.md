# H222

Microsoft Threat Intelligence detailed **GigaWiper**, a destructive backdoor assembled from multiple malware families (reusing Crucio ransomware and FlockWiper code) that reduces hosts to an unbootable, unrecoverable state. The durable hunting angle is not GigaWiper's binary — it is the small set of destructive disk primitives any wiper of this class must perform: opening raw physical-drive handles and overwriting content, and issuing `IOCTL_DISK_CREATE_DISK` to destroy partition structures. HEARTH covered the encryption (T1486) and recovery-inhibition (T1490) angles of destructive tradecraft but had no coverage for the disk-wipe techniques themselves.

## Why

- **Raw-disk overwriting is the irreversible act, and it is loud in telemetry.** A user-mode process opening `\\.\PhysicalDrive` and issuing 10 MB sequential writes is rare and high-signal; Sysmon EID 9 (RawAccessRead) and process-create events capture it without any new sensor.
- **`IOCTL_DISK_CREATE_DISK` on non-boot drives is a near-unambiguous structure-wipe signal.** Outside of provisioning and imaging tooling, almost nothing legitimately reinitializes partition tables on multiple drives — and provisioning tools are signed, run from expected paths, and do not chain into a forced reboot.
- **The chain distinguishes destruction from administration.** Drive enumeration (`Win32_DiskDrive`) → raw writes / `IOCTL_DISK_CREATE_DISK` → boot-file re-permission and deletion → forced zero-delay reboot is a wiper's fingerprint; any single step is benign, but the ordered sequence from one untrusted process is not.
- **Coverage gap for destructive impact.** HEARTH lacked T1561.x entirely; wipers (GigaWiper, and the broader FlockWiper/HermeticWiper/CaddyWiper lineage) are a recurring, high-consequence threat where minutes of early detection matter.
