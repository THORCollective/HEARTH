# H153

Threat actors are exploiting AD CS templates marked `ENROLLEE_SUPPLIES_SUBJECT` with `Client Authentication` EKU and manager-approval-disabled (ESC1) — using Certify or Certipy to request certificates with an arbitrary `Subject Alternative Name` impersonating a Domain Admin, then authenticating via PKINIT.

## Why
- ESC1 is the highest-impact AD CS misconfiguration that still ships in many environments — a single template with `ENROLLEE_SUPPLIES_SUBJECT` + a broad enrollment ACL is a direct path from low-privileged domain user to Domain Admin, and Unit 42 reported active exploitation in May 2026
- The mismatch between requester identity and SAN is a near-zero-FP anchor — a legitimate cert request issued for `alice@corp.local` should be requested by `alice`, not by `bob`; a deviation is virtually always abuse or misconfiguration that warrants investigation either way
- Most environments do NOT enable AD CS audit logging by default — establishing the hunt forces the prerequisite work of turning on `4886`/`4887` and the OS-level CertSvc audit subcategory, which is a defensive win independent of catching this specific attacker
- The Certify and Certipy toolchain leaves distinctive command-line patterns (`/altname:`, `/upn:`, `req --upn`) that survive renames; combined with the SAN-vs-requester mismatch this becomes a robust, multi-signal hunt
