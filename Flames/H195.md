# H195

As part of its masquerading strategy (ESET maps this to T1036.001), the Gentlemen group ships its EDR-killer drivers and user-mode components with fabricated version information and invalid digital signatures copied from legitimate vendor executables — the binary claims to be signed but fails validation. The kernel drivers it abuses (BYOVD) are themselves either legitimately signed but known-vulnerable (`dmx.sys` Zemana WatchDog CVE-2022-42045, IObit `IMFForceDelete` CVE-2025-26125, `nseckrnl.sys`, `GameDriverX64.sys`, `360netmon_wfp.sys`, `googleApiUtil64.sys`, `ThrottleBlood.sys`, `havoc.sys`) — many carrying certificates that have since been revoked or abused — or are custom rootkits (`eb.sys`, `G11.sys`/PoisonX) named to look legitimate. We hypothesize that hunting driver-load events whose signature is Unsigned, Invalid, Expired, or chains to a revoked/abused code-signing certificate — especially when the file path or name impersonates a known vendor but the cert does not validate — surfaces these dropped drivers regardless of which specific driver is loaded. Paired with [[H194]] (the service-install + load chain that delivers the same drivers).

## Why
- ESET explicitly ties Gentlemen to T1036.001 (invalid signatures copied from legitimate executables) and to BYOVD; signature status is therefore a direct, behavior-anchored pivot rather than a guess.
- Kernel drivers are one of the few file classes where Windows still loads improperly-signed or revoked-cert images under certain conditions, so an Invalid/Revoked `SignatureStatus` on a loaded driver is both rare and high-fidelity.
- Sysmon EID 6 with `<CheckRevocation/>` catches the common BYOVD pattern of a once-valid cert that has since been revoked — the exact lifecycle of abused anti-cheat and utility-driver certs in this campaign.
- Cross-referencing the loaded driver hash against LOLDrivers turns this into a maintainable, hash-rotating-resistant hunt that fires on the kernel foothold before EDR is blinded.
